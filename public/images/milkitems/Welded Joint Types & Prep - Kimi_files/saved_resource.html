Introduction & Standards

Understanding the framework of structural steel welding

What is Welding?

Welding is a fabrication process that joins materials (typically metals or thermoplastics) by causing coalescence. In structural steel construction, welding is the primary method for creating permanent connections between members.

🔥 Importance in Structural Steel

  • • Creates rigid, continuous connections
  • • Eliminates need for bolt holes (no section reduction)
  • • Provides excellent fatigue resistance
  • • Allows complex geometric configurations
  • • Cost-effective for mass production

Speaker Notes: Emphasize that AISC references AWS D1.1 for all welding requirements. ASTM provides the "what" (material), AISC provides the "how" (design), and AWS provides the "how-to" (execution). Mention that AWS D1.1 is the most widely used welding code in the world for structural steel.

Types of Welded Joints

Five fundamental joint configurations in structural steel welding

B

Butt Joint

Two plates in same plane, joined edge-to-edge with groove weld

→ Beam & Column Splices

L

Lap Joint

One plate overlaps another, joined with fillet welds

→ Cover Plates, Bracing

T

Tee Joint

One member at 90° to another, forming a "T" shape

→ Stiffeners, Base Plates

C

Corner Joint

Two members at 90° forming an L-shaped corner

→ Box Sections, Frames

E

Edge Joint

Parallel plate edges side by side; minimal structural load

→ Sheet Metal, Covers

Speaker Notes: Butt joints provide the highest strength because the weld metal replaces the base metal across the full cross-section. Lap joints are economical but create eccentricity. Tee joints are the most common in building construction. Corner joints are essential for box girders and hollow sections. Edge joints are rarely used for primary structural loads.

Joint Preparation

Essential steps before welding per AISC/AWS D1.1 requirements

Pre-Weld Requirements

🧹 Surface Cleaning

  • Remove rust, mill scale, oil, grease
  • Eliminate paint, moisture, and contaminants
  • Grind or blast clean to bright metal (SSPC-SP10)

📐 Edge Preparation

  • Machine or flame-cut groove edges
  • Maintain proper groove angle (per WPS)
  • Ensure correct root opening dimension
  • Provide root face (land) when specified

📏 Alignment & Fit-Up

  • Align plates accurately within tolerance
  • Check root gap with feeler gauges
  • Use clamps, jigs, or fixtures
  • Apply tack welds at proper spacing

🌡️ Preheat & Interpass

  • Apply preheat per AWS D1.1 Table 3.2
  • Control interpass temperature
  • Use backing bars when specified
  • Select proper electrode/wire (F-number)

Speaker Notes: Joint preparation is the single most important factor affecting weld quality. AWS D1.1 Section 5 requires all surfaces to be free of contaminants. Preheat is mandatory for thicker sections and higher strength steels to prevent hydrogen-induced cracking (cold cracking). Always reference the approved Welding Procedure Specification (WPS) for exact parameters.

Table 2: Comprehensive Joint Comparison
Joint Type Weld Used Strength Advantages Applications
Butt Groove Weld 🔴 Very High Full section continuity, no eccentricity, excellent fatigue resistance Beam splices, column splices, bridges, pressure vessels
Lap Fillet Weld 🟡 Moderate Simple preparation, no edge bevel, easy to fit-up Thin plates, cover plates, bracing, repair work

Speaker Notes: Double-V and double-U grooves reduce weld metal volume by ~50% compared to single-sided grooves on thick plates, minimizing distortion and cost. U-grooves require special cutting equipment but save significant filler metal. J-grooves are used when one side has limited access. Always consider distortion control when selecting groove type — symmetrical double-sided welds minimize angular distortion.

Structural Steel Fabrication

Common butt joint groove configurations used in structural fabrication

Key Application Insights

🏢 Buildings (AISC 360)

  • Moment connections: CJP groove welds
  • Shear connections: Fillet welds or PJP
  • Seismic: Demand critical welds per AISC 341

🌉 Bridges (AASHTO/AWS D1.5)

  • Fracture critical members: FC welding
  • Tub girders: Corner joints with full penetration
  • Splice plates: Butt joints with backing

⚗️ Pressure Vessels (ASME Section VIII)

  • Category A joints: Double-welded butt
  • RT/UT required for all butt welds
  • Post-weld heat treatment (PWHT) mandatory
Speaker Notes: In seismic zones (AISC 341), demand critical welds require special toughness properties (Charpy V-notch at 0°F or -20°F). Bridge welding follows AWS D1.5, which has stricter requirements than D1.1. Offshore structures (API RP 2Z) require CTOD testing for thick sections. Always match the welding code to the application — never use D1.1 for pressure vessels (use ASME IX).

AISC & ASTM Requirements

Design standards and material specifications for structural welding

AISC Design Requirements

📐 Structural Design (AISC 360)

  • LRFD & ASD design methodologies
  • Weld strength: φ = 0.75 (LRFD), Ω = 2.00 (ASD)
  • Fillet weld effective throat = 0.707 × weld size
  • Groove weld strength = base metal strength (CJP)
  • Minimum weld size per Table J2.4
  • Maximum spacing for intermittent fillet welds

🔍 Inspection & Quality (AISC 360 Ch. N)

  • Visual inspection (VT) — 100% of welds
  • MT/PT for surface defects (when specified)
  • UT/RT for volumetric defects (CJP welds)
  • Inspector qualifications per AWS D1.1
  • Nondestructive testing (NDT) acceptance criteria

⚡ Seismic Provisions (AISC 341)

  • Demand critical welds: CVN toughness ≥ 20 ft-lb @ 0°F
  • Protected zones: No weld tabs, repairs limited
  • Backing bars: Removal required for seismic welds
  • Weld access holes per approved geometry

📋 Welding Consumables

  • A5.1: Carbon steel electrodes (E6010, E7018)
  • A5.18: Carbon steel filler metals (ER70S-6)
  • A5.20: Self-shielded flux-cored (E71T-1, E71T-8)
  • Filler metal strength must match or exceed base metal
Speaker Notes: AISC 360 Chapter J covers connections, with Section J2 specifically addressing welds. The strength reduction factor φ = 0.75 applies to all welds. For seismic, AISC 341 Supp. 1 requires weld metal with specific diffusible hydrogen limits (H8 or lower). ASTM A992 is the most common material for W-shapes in the US — it replaced A572 Gr.50 for rolled shapes. Always verify filler metal classification matches the WPS and base metal strength.

Conclusion & References

Summary of key takeaways and authoritative sources

Key Takeaways
  • Different welded joints serve different structural purposes — select based on load path and accessibility
  • Proper joint preparation is the foundation of weld quality and structural integrity
  • AISC provides the design framework for safe, economical welded connections
  • ASTM ensures material consistency and traceability through standardized testing
  • AWS D1.1 governs execution — from WPS to final inspection
  • Following these standards ensures safe, durable, and reliable welded structures

🎯 Remember

Design per AISC → Material per ASTM → Weld per AWS → Inspect per Code. Any break in this chain compromises structural safety.
